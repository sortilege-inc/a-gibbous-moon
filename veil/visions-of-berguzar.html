Visions of Bergüzar

☾
Spoilers, and prep — not canon. The complete dream-cycle as written. The party has lived only a handful of these at the table (the desert, the training camp, the harem naming, and the final catastrophe); the rest are yours to deal out. Run one per long rest, in order for clarity or shuffled for mystery, and finish the cycle before The Grand Negotiation.

Across the campaign the Haseki Sultan reaches into the party's sleep to tell them her life — not as memory but as a plea. Fifteen dreams carry a forest child from a broken clock to the throne of the world's end. This is the whole of it, in sequence, with the reveal buried in each.

I · The Meeting

A wooden house deep in the forest that "wants to be remembered." A woman scrubs the floor humming the Mother's Song; her small daughter dances behind her and knocks her father's fine clock — the one expensive thing they own, name Lukas inscribed on it — to the floor. As the girl weeps over the pieces, they knit themselves back together in her hands, glowing, mending — no known school of magic. Her mother's whispered name for her: Karya.

II · The Dance of Time

The same house, a season warmer. Clocks wind and ring uncontrollably through the atelier; Karya covers her ears and flees inside, her father chasing after. The gift is growing, and it frightens the house that holds it.

III · Tears of Death

Karya's sister Mira huddles crying over a dead kitten. Karya lifts it, eyes rolling white, and pours life back into it — time-manipulation magic only the strongest arcanists can work. The kitten purrs; Mira is amazed, unafraid. Their father is not: he storms in — "There will be no glowing lights under my roof" — seizes Karya, raises his hand to strike, and drops dead atop her, killed by her raw power. Blood-painted and calm, she slides him aside. Her mother watches with a lifeless face and purple eyes: enchanted, forced into a serene smile, holding a broken clock.

IV · The Screams

Karya gathers her things to leave forever. On the porch Mira shrieks "You murderous witch! Get out, go!" — and as she screams, a flicker of light leaves her eyes and the yard's stones tremble and rise: Mira, too, has the gift (transmutation, like Thaumaturgy). The mother's bitter screams fill the air. Karya murmurs "I love you, kitten" to her sister and walks away as the rocks lift free of the ground. This parting — sister against sister, both diviners of the same blood — is the root of everything.

V · Alone

Karya survives alone in the forest by a rushing stream, feeding a small fire, terrified of the coming dark and her own empty stomach. Drawn toward distant lights, she hears the faint clash of swords. (Played at the table as the cold desert dream that left the party exhausted.)

VI · The First Glance

She spies two men sparring under a crimson sunset before Ottoman flags: a richly dressed boy of sixteen — Abd-ul Mejid — and his stoic mentor, Alemdar Pasha, who quotes Rumi ("A wound is the place where enlightenment enters you") and scolds the prince's haste. Bored, Mejid catches Karya's eye across the treeline; the attraction is instant. He seizes the opening to land a divine smite on his tutor. Alemdar counters Karya's panicked fireball into harmless snowflakes — a Counterspell — and calls after her: "Remember that we do not use arcane among the people, my Shahzadah." (The party has lived this one as the military-camp dream.)

VII · A Moment of Intimacy

Mejid follows her to her camp, sits a careful distance away, and coaxes out her story. She weeps; he lays his cape over her shoulders and presses a sigil-printed paper into her hand — his Shahzadah's seal, a way to reach him. She cannot read it. She folds it against her heart.

VIII · Saviour

In a village coffee-house, Karya asks the owner to read the paper. He lies — claims it owes her five gold, pockets it — and her innate divining power flares: he's lying, and she can feel he means to keep it for his own daughter. A mustachioed man rises, snatches the letter back, and bows: "My name is Aziz Sefa. I am a cadet at the military academy." He takes her to the Sublime Porte — to Istanbul. (This is why Aziz is the one man Bergüzar trusts as a brother — and why his turn against Mira cuts so deep.)

IX · Welcome to the Harem

Clean, grown, beautiful, Karya joins a circle of young women in a ceremony as the crown prince watches from a balcony. Servants bring hookahs; with every draw, charms of light bloom under the women's skin, resolving into the Eye of Solomon — "the Pact and the Justice Brought." The pact that binds the Harem sorceresses to the throne is being sealed. (Lived at the table during the cistern rest.)

X · The Gardens

An older instructress schools the circle: "Learn to hold your anger… Your words hold the most power, not the destruction you can cause. Remember Hürrem Sultan, remember Kösem Sultan." By order of Padishah Mahmud II they will learn to "gaze upon the world and the future, with eyes spying." Karya speaks the teacher's words a breath before she does, then levitates as stones, flowers, and a distant clock-tower's hands spin at her power — beyond the normal measurements of arcane. The teacher names her anew: Bergüzar. (Also lived, as the naming dream.)

XI · The Miracle Child

Present day. Bergüzar and Mejid, married and in love, take coffee on a Bosphorus balcony. She reads his overturned cup, eyes rolling white, and returns with a sour smile: "We are with child. An heir, your majesty." He leaps up in joy; she hugs him back — and hides a creeping horror behind a sly smile. She already knows what the child becomes.

XII · The Source

Bergüzar and Mejid descend to a pitch-black cell to consult the caged Shahmeran. Bergüzar can feel untapped arcane in herself she cannot release; she demands to know how. The serpent answers with the secret that shapes the whole campaign — a diviner's power cannot be shared by another of the same blood — and names the price without naming her: Mira. (See Secret III.)

XIII · Destruction

The same balcony, the view undone: an ashen future past the nineteenth century, Istanbul ringed by enemy fleets and waiting aircraft. Bergüzar runs to the throne room to find her own grown son — Mejid's image with her features — smiling with glowing eyes. "Don't do this." He tightens his fists: "I wish…" The sea rises to swallow the ships, the city cracks open, everyone screams and dies, and the dreamer falls into darkness. (Mechanically: on waking, a failed DC 13 Wisdom save leaves the dreamer frightened of magic for an hour.) This is the catastrophe every other secret serves. (The party shared this dream on the eve of sailing to Mira.)

Running the cycle

One dream per long rest is the intended pace, completed before the party reaches The Grand Negotiation. Kept in order, the dreams read as a life; shuffled, they become a mystery the players must assemble — the runaway girl (I–V), the prince and the pact (VI–X), the horror and its source (XI–XII), and the end it all points to (XIII). However you deal them, the players should reach the Negotiation understanding that the Haseki Sultan is not an enemy to be toppled but a woman asking for help — and that helping her may mean deciding the fate of the child she carries.
