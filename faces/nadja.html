The Folk of the City

Nadja

Nadja is a woman in her thirties, self-possessed in her governance of the kitchen at a mosque's soup-kitchen in Constantinople. Practical and mindful of her community, she oversees the volunteers, orders the preparing of the meals, and deals with a warm hand alike with the children and the grown folk who come to her; approachable, she assists all who are in want, distributing food and offering shelter once the meal is done, and yet she keeps a wary eye, and is slow to trust the unbidden offers of strangers.

During one meal-service Nadja drew members of the company into her work, enquiring after a man last seen at the soup-line some few days before. Her devotion to her neighbours shows besides in her taste for questions of theology, and above all in the matter of whether the practices of Islam might embrace the magically arisen who are not human; in thoughtful converse with Gizem Faruki she turned over the spiritual estate of such diverse beings, and confided her concern for a friend seeking guidance upon these very things.

Her forward and helpful nature shows again in her counsel that the company seek out Kore, a being dwelling in the Undercity, to treat of spiritual communication; and her grasp of local lore is plain when she names Nazif's shop as a gateway into the Undercity, a bridge for those who would come to an understanding of their faith. By this compassionate temper Nadja not only discharges her duties at the soup-kitchen but fosters, into the bargain, a genuine converse upon matters of spirit and of inclusion within her community.
