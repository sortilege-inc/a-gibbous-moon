The Folk of the City

Alemdar Mustafa Pasha

Alemdar Mustafa Pasha is a bandaged figure who shows the world nothing but his eyes, and is reputed to be the dead commander of the Sekban. He is a man of tactics and cold composure, ever poised for the bloodshed that may come; his hands are bound tight in magical wrappings that preserve his own person and shield those about him. To the Sekban he belongs, and it is said of him that he bridges the worlds above and below.

When he met the company, Alemdar greeted Gizem Faruki by name, so betraying that their coming was no surprise to him, and voiced his approval of their deeds as Sharif the Strong Fur, a cat and go-between, had reported them. He asked whether they had been followed, and spoke of the need for a common front with Ahra 'the Wolf' of the Janissaries, their old enmity notwithstanding; he begged them carry terms to her, urging that unity served the Empire and all who dwell in it. He made mention of a young tiefling, Shehrazat, afflicted with the blood rot, and asked Sidra al-Najjar to visit and to help her if she could; and he set before them a leather-tied journal of Osman's, which he had taken but could not decipher, for their use.

His office kept a fine longsword, fit for battle, hung behind the desk. He held to a policy of studied misdirection, counselling the party to preserve the show of an undying Janissary–Sekban enmity for the confounding of watching eyes, and inviting them to send him recruits, and any versed in the elements or in alchemy, to swell the ranks of the "new Sekban Army."

By other accounts Alemdar Mustafa Pasha is a winged thing, a being at once beautiful and dreadful. This figure takes the likeness of a woman — spindly of limb, her hair somehow indistinct — and troubles all who behold her. She is named an albasti, one of those creatures that feed upon the dreams of the sleeping and prey upon the defenceless; and her face is barred by a vent of flesh where nose and mouth should be, which lends her a horror all her own.

Within the hidden reaches of the Undercity the Alemdar Mustafa Pasha holds great sway, honoured for keeping order among its people and for guarding its children against the gangs that would hunt them. Her name is spoken in the streets with familiarity and regard, a sign of the office she fills there. To the Sekban soldiers she is joined — that corps remembered for the breaking of the Janissaries — and she goes clad in the uniforms of that disbanded body; a warren of connected buildings in the Undercity answers to her hand.

In one late encounter the Alemdar Mustafa Pasha showed herself in a dim alley, taking the hand of a human accomplice as she stalked her chosen prey. The air hung thick with an unnatural dread that left the bystanders powerless to stir. She would have drawn Ahmet into sleep by passing her hand across his eyes and so into a dream; but her hunting broke into open struggle, and Ahmet and his fellows fought back hard, and in the end laid the albasti low.

Earlier still she had given warning to Rufus Dimitrious, bidding him "seek out the Alemdar Mustafa Pasha" as she swept past him — a hint of her power over what men perceive, and of the workings she commands; in one performance her song was seen to weave a spell of Darkness, betraying her arcane arts. Now Kore has told the party that she has treated with the Alemdar Mustafa Pasha to secure safe lodging for a band of children lately in the grip of a gang-lord named Mr. Karen, at a price of twenty gold that the party wins from a defeated dwarf. So provided, the children are sheltered and kept, which sets her yet more firmly as a protector and provider in the Undercity, a lawful authority amid the gangs' misrule.

When the fight with Ahmet was done, the company weighed the still body of the albasti and made it fast for carriage to a certain patron, that it might be appraised — a turning-point in their long road, tangled as it is with this enigmatic yet guardian presence.

In the wake of these doings the Alemdar Mustafa Pasha put Nazif, a perfumist, to death, charging him with betrayal and declaring that his charge was passing to the Council of the Ninth Life. A state of emergency followed from the Bostanji Imperial Guard, and the crowd scattered in confusion. After the killing Sidra al-Najjar came forward to ask an audience; the answer was, "Now is not the time, little one, but soon," and then she led her train away as the guards restored order, her people melting into the city's shadows. Gizem Faruki afterward remarked that the Alemdar Mustafa Pasha's followers wore Sekban uniforms, and so tied them to a history of violence; it was reported, too, that she had relieved Nazif of his duties. Her deeds set tongues wagging of the city's unrest — the weapon-thefts, the protests — and the party was charged to look into the disturbance about the ruins of Saint Benoit Church, where her workings pressed upon the state of the unquiet dead.

Of late she has been linked to a journal that Osman pronounces a forgery, whence a meeting was set in the Undercity, at the Reef Tavern, to obtain the true book. The place is well known to the party, and Osman shows plain reluctance to venture into the Undercity — a sign, perhaps, that the Alemdar Mustafa Pasha is both dangerous and given to guile. The company weighed what it might mean to make common cause with Osman and with her, whose presence remains veiled in mystery, a blend of old grievance and coming intrigue rooted in the Undercity. Her brother, moreover, Radu the Handsome, gave the party his thanks for freeing and destroying him — a token that she had once been bound or imprisoned; and upon her release she said her burden was lifted and that she would depart, as though some old entanglement had at last reached its close.

She has been named, besides, in the matter of a sinking ship, a fleeing man speaking of her, which marks her part in weighty events. Her doings may cross those of sundry factions — rival treasure-hunters, and the faction of A Certain Community, which sets itself against all plurality and seeks to smother the magical orders. What she wants, and what she means, grows only more interesting to those who must deal with so shadowed a figure.

Alemdar Pasha is also a senior commander of the Turkish arms, renowned alike for his martial skill and his philosophy. He is bound to a younger Padishah Abd-ul Mejid, the Padishah that was to be, and preaches discipline and an enlightenment won through the training of the body: that wounds bring wisdom, and that a universal rapture is reached by disciplined precision. He rebukes Padishah Abd-ul Mejid for turning magic against their own people, holding fast to the proprieties of combat.

In a shared dream he schools the young Padishah Abd-ul Mejid in the worth of focus and the cost of distraction, showing his authority and his wisdom, and turning aside a magical stroke of Padishah Abd-ul Mejid's — proof of his command of both the arcane and the martial.

The Ghost of March is an entity woven into the underworld and the political machinations of Istanbul. It has a tie to the Janissaries and pleads for open-handed alliances, above all with those who are not human. Alemdar Mustafa Pasha has had his part in the party's affairs, sending them to find Alexander and voicing his concern for the welfare of Osman Hamdi Bey. In one exchange he started at the name "Shahmeran," which marked its uncommon power and peril. He lies bound beneath the Basilica Cistern since the days of Justinian and Theodora, and asked the party to recover the sword lately turned against his life, describing it in full; the company knew it for the twin of a blade set out on show at the Janissary headquarters.
