Padishah Abd-ul Mejid

Padishah Abd-ul Mejid, the crown prince of the Ottoman Empire, is a figure marked by a unique blend of traditional and modern attire, exuding a noble bearing and often flanked by well-dressed, armed guards. As he participates in a ceremonial parade leading to his enthronement as Padishah, he is presented with the Sword of Osman at the steps of the Hagia Sophia, a powerful symbol of his ascension to power. The crowd's reactions are mixed, as some attendees express dissent through the act of throwing coins, invoking the legacy of the janissaries, whose lingering public sentiment raises the alertness among Abd-ul Mejid's security.

During the parade, Abd-ul Mejid encounters Osman Hamdi Bey, a key ally with whom he shares a relationship that is respectful but possibly distant. Together, they navigate the challenges of significant political transitions, including the confirmation of the Caliph's death. Abd-ul Mejid shows foresight in seeking assistance as he faces the weight of new responsibilities. While aboard the Ejder-i Derya, he discusses recent calamities affecting the ship and the political climate, signaling a proactive commitment to the welfare of the Empire.

Recently, Abd-ul Mejid has been elevated to the status of a new Pasha and has entered into marriage with Osman, a character of substantial influence. Though this union does not appear to serve as a strategic alliance with another head of state, Osman's private meetings with Abd-ul Mejid suggest a close relationship that intertwines both state and personal matters. Amid ongoing palace intrigues, rumors have arisen regarding Abd-ul Mejid’s potential connections to magical dealings or the undercity, though these claims remain unverified.

Abd-ul Mejid is also involved in the construction of a new palace and extends an invitation to Osman and her recommended associates for a meeting that holds importance for the political landscape, hinting at a connection to the Kavalan’s Rebellion. His invitation, laden with hidden thieves' cant, signifies a secretive nature and highlights his tactical demeanor in preparation for potential conflicts. He is recognized as a leader with ties to the Sekban and a history of navigating the complex dynamics within the Empire, particularly regarding the tensions with the Janissaries.

Alongside his noble stature, there exists a younger, different aspect of this lineage. Abd-ul Mejid, known affectionately as Padishah Abd-ul Mejid, is also a youthful boy of approximately 15 or 16 years old, currently training under Alemdar Mustafa Pasha. He demonstrates curiosity and awareness during his training sessions, though he faces reprimands from his mentor for distractions. Padishah Abd-ul Mejid is noted for his budding magical abilities, which, while impressive, have drawn criticism for use against their own people. Through a shared dream, he learns valuable lessons about the nature of wounds and enlightenment, absorbing the wisdom of Rumi that emphasizes that "a wound is the place where Enlightenment enters you."

In the latest developments, Abd-ul Mejid is noted for his firm stance against pluralism in governance, asserting the necessity for a strong, singular voice. He is also engaged in diplomatic negotiations between the Ottoman Empire and Egypt, presiding over a formal diplomatic meeting where he announces the capture of Hamit the Quartz of Damascus, an associate of Kavalan. During this meeting, he displays signs of relief and disturbance, indicating awareness of deeper issues within the palace. Abd-ul Mejid witnesses the execution of Hamit by Kavalan, who slits Hamit's throat and casts a spell on him to extract information. This act publicly implicates the Topkapi Palace in potential foul play, straining the diplomatic talks. He observes an Unidentified Palace Official leaving the talks in haste, suggesting the presence of a mole or co-conspirator within the palace. His complex duality as both a noble figure and an emerging young man heralds a future filled with potential influence and power within the turbulent political climate of the Empire.
