Ruins of Troy

---

aliases:

  • "Ruins of \"Troy\""

---

The Ruins of Troy lie nestled against rolling hills near a farmhouse outside Constantinople, a site steeped in mystery and ancient history. This partially collapsed stone chamber, reminiscent of a Megaron great hall, features a central hearth and columns, with a crumbling northern wall that opens into an ominous darkness. The air carries a faint scent of sulfur, hinting at its subterranean nature, while the walls are adorned with pre-Greek frescoes that whisper tales of a bygone era. The ground around the ruins is marked by a 30-foot radius of desaturated yet vibrant life, drawing the attention of local inhabitants and adventurers alike.

Among those drawn to the site is a young boy named Ahmet Yusuf, who discovers the peculiar tree atop the hill, while Sidra al-Najjar, a mage, detects faint magical residues in the farmhouse nearby. Jean-Zera Mongé, another member of the group, identifies lingering transmutation magic associated with the tree, suggesting deeper connections to the ruins. Their exploration leads them to a locked study within the farmhouse, where they uncover literature focused on Troy, including a hidden parchment titled “The Lion’s King,” a spell for unlocking doors.

Venturing further, the party navigates through a narrow crevice into the cave, revealing the grand hall's architectural layout, which indicates its former use as a throne room. Here, they encounter an 8-10-foot-long serpent that emerges from the shadows, warning them of the dangers posed by the chamber and demanding the removal of "interlopers." This encounter leads to the discovery of two scholars, Frank Calvert and Heinrich Schliemann, who are found frozen in a luminous ward before a massive door adorned with imagery of Aphrodite and snake-handled pulls. As the ward begins to release its hold, Schliemann expresses an urgent desire to open the door, claiming it leads to the ancient city of Troy.

The tension escalates as the party grapples with the implications of their findings, the significance of the artifacts, and the warnings of the serpent. The Ruins of Troy stand as a pivotal location, rich with archaeological significance and fraught with the potential for discovery and danger, beckoning adventurers to uncover its secrets.
