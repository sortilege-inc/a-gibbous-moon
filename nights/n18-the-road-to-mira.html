Night 18 · Interlude · 2026-07-07

The Road to Mira

Summary

The battle for the Crown of Sargon ended atop the cloud-wreathed ziggurat with the party battered but victorious. Gizem Faruki clung to the black dragon's brow, hammering it with stunning strikes; Miray Ruba Mata Biru and Jean-Zera Mongé, both dropped by its acid breath, dragged themselves back into the fight with moonbeam and mockery. The turning point, though, was the crown itself. Sidra al-Najjar set it upon her brow in the thick of the fray, and the artifact answered with two rebounds: first a fog of memory that left her, Memento-like, unable to recall how she had come to be fighting a dragon at all; then something far greater. The djinn that had lived inside her for as long as she could remember was wrenched bodily out of her, taking shape at her back as a being unto itself. Newly attuned to the crown and steadied by its authority, Sidra spoke a single word of command, and the dragon — down to its last breath — turned and fled into the clouds. The crown was theirs, and the tower let them go.

The change in Sidra ran deeper than any wound. Where the djinn had once worn her like a hermit-crab's shell, now she held it, in its own words, as one holds a leash. It gave her a name at last — Feravi, "the imagined" — and confessed itself grown fond of her over their years together, though it would promise only not to harm her, leaving the question of her friends conspicuously unanswered.

Resting afterward at Osman Hamdi Bey's house, the company turned to the meaning of their shared dreams. Poring over the visions, Jean-Zera Mongé pieced it together: the dreams were no gift and no warning, but a rebound — a psychic bleed-over caught when the four of them had first passed near Mira's home by the archaeological dig, where the diviner's magic and her sister Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun's were locked in a struggle carried in their shared blood. Whether the visions were true events, or Bergüzar's own coloured memories, or something further removed still, none of them could say.

While they made ready to sail — Miray Ruba Mata Biru working the bathhouses for palace gossip, Gizem Faruki passing a Sufi woman's appeal up toward the Harem, Jean-Zera Mongé reading Osman's diary and confirming the dig had only ever been a pretext to find Mira — a last and darkest dream came the night before they left. They looked out again over the Bosphorus, but on an ashen, ruined future: foreign ships on the horizon, aircraft in a grey sky, all of it bearing ill. Bergüzar rushed to the throne room, where her own son sat crowned in his father's place, and begged him, "Don't do this." He only smiled and began to say, "I wish—" and the dreamers woke in a cold sweat to an after-image of the sea rising to swallow the ships, the city cracking open, and darkness falling over all. It was, the mages among them knew, the working of a Wish — magic at its very uttermost.

They crossed by ship in a day and a half and struck out overland toward Mira's farmhouse. But they were not the only ones bound for her door. In the trees near the old dig site, riflemen of the Bostanji Imperial Guard opened fire, and Sidra al-Najjar's new-found sight into minds named their leader: Aziz Sefa Bey, come to kill Mira before the party could reach her. Gizem caught bullets from the air and flung them back, rode down the shooters, and knocked their heads together; Miray's moonbeam, Jean-Zera's whispers, and Sidra's evil eye did the rest. Shaken and outmatched, Aziz worked a ring into a cloud of fog and vanished, and his surviving soldiers stood down. The way to Mira lay open — and the race for her was now in the open too.

Preparations and palace intelligence - The company readied a voyage to Mira while probing the affairs of the palace and the Sultan.

  • Bathhouse gossip on an heir - Miray Ruba Mata Biru worked her old bathhouse contacts and picked up palace servants' guarded excitement over a pregnancy and a possible heir "not a blood relation" to them—hints of a coming child tied to the palace.
  • The Padishah's leanings - The talk placed Padishah Abd-ul Mejid as leaning toward openness—to magic, to non-humans, to modernization—though he had not solidified a position, while traditionalists pushed back and Aziz's faction pressed its own line.
  • A message toward the Harem - Gizem Faruki arranged for a written appeal—framed as a Sufi women's plea for protection and a question of where the Sultan stood—to be relayed up toward the head of the Harem.
  • Osman's diary read - Jean-Zera Mongé read Osman Hamdi Bey's diary and confirmed the archaeological dig had been almost entirely a pretext; Osman's true purpose had been to find Mira, whose existence he had pieced together from fragments the tight-lipped Bergüzar let slip.

  • Gizem turns the guns - Gizem Faruki caught bullets from the air and flung them back with lethal force, rode down the Bostanji Imperial Guard shooters, knocked two of them senseless head-to-head, and disarmed another—her body moving of its own divine instinct.
  • The party breaks the ambush - Miray Ruba Mata Biru's moonbeam, Jean-Zera Mongé's dissonant whispers and shield, and Sidra al-Najjar's hellish rebuke and evil eye downed several of the guardsmen and their secret-service escort.
  • Aziz escapes in fog - Shaken and outmatched, Aziz Sefa Bey worked a ring into a fog like a cloud and vanished; his surviving soldiers stood down and let the party pass, leaving the road to Mira open.

Moments

  • Memento on the Ziggurat: Sidra al-Najjar set the Crown of Sargon on her head in the middle of the dragon-fight, and the world fell out from under her memory. Like the man in the film who cannot recall why he is standing in the diner, she found herself looking at a downed Miray Ruba Mata Biru and a black dragon banking toward her with no notion of how she had come to be there at all — only that, apparently, she got into shenanigans.
  • The Djinn Torn Loose: The crown's second rebound did what nothing ever had: it pulled the djinn out of her. It felt, Sidra al-Najjar said, like a punch that sends the spirit flying out the other side — a sudden cold nakedness where a companion of years had been — and then there it stood at her back, larger than life, kind-faced, a being at last unto itself. Its first act was fury on her behalf at the dragon that had hurt her.
  • You Showed Him Who's Boss: Newly crowned and steadied, Sidra al-Najjar simply told the dragon to stop, and it did — folding its wings and settling before them with a ferocious, baffled look. A word more and it fled into the clouds with nine hit points to its name. "You survived and thrived and took the crown and attuned to it," said the Keeper, "and didn't even kill the dragon — which, to me, is a superior victory."
  • A Home, and Then a Leash: Asked what had changed between them, the freed djinn found the words. "You were a home that I had made," it told Sidra al-Najjar, "in the way you have seen those crabs that take on a shell. You were a friend — and now you are holding a leash." It had grown fond of her, it said, and meant her no harm. That it said nothing at all about her friends did not go unnoticed.
  • Feravi, the Imagined: The djinn gave its name for the first time — Feravi, "the imagined" — and took the shape, more or less, of a larger-than-life man with a kind face and a shirt left a little too open. It was strange, it allowed, but not distressing. "We may still get up to some mischief, however."
  • The Wrong Place at the Wrong Time: Studying the dreams, Jean-Zera Mongé finally understood: they were nobody's message. When the four of them had first passed the estate by the dig, they had walked through the backwash of a fight — the diviner Mira and her sister Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun straining against each other through their shared blood — and caught the psychic spillover full in the face. How many levels removed from the truth the visions were, no one could say.
  • "I Wish—": The last dream showed a grey and broken future — foreign ships and aircraft in an ashen sky — and Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun running to a throne room where her own son sat crowned. "Don't do this," she begged him; he only smiled and said, "I wish—," and the dreamers woke drenched in cold sweat to an after-image of the sea rising to swallow the fleets, the city splitting open, and darkness taking everything.
  • No Birds: Something was wrong on the road to Mira before anyone drew a weapon. Miray Ruba Mata Biru, used to the noise of the city, marked the silence first — the conspicuous absence of any birdsong — and then, in a low branch, a man with a rifle already sighted on Jean-Zera Mongé.
  • The Mind Behind the Guns: With bullets cracking from the trees, Sidra al-Najjar reached into the leader's thoughts through the crown and found him calm, unhurried, mildly surprised they had come this far — and certain his men would finish them. Then she found the rest: this was Aziz Sefa Bey, and he was on his way to kill Mira.
  • Right Enchilada: A bullet meant for Gizem Faruki never landed — she caught it from the air, spun it, and flung it back hard enough to nearly knock its shooter out of his tree, all of it pure pre-conscious instinct. Muttering her "right enchilada" under her breath, she carried a divine favour through the rest of the fight, catching rounds and knocking guardsmen's heads together like something out of an adventure serial.
  • A Slow Learner: Aziz Sefa Bey walked calmly into the middle of the firefight with his cane and monocle and put two shots at Jean-Zera Mongé's feet — not even trying to kill. "I would have thought you would have learned from our last meeting," he said. "It's good that I'm a slow learner," Jean-Zera answered — and by the end it was Aziz, shaken and outmatched, who worked a ring into a cloud of fog and disappeared, leaving the road to Mira open at last.
