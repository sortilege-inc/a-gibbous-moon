Night 18 · Interlude · 2026-07-07

The Road to Mira

Summary

The battle for the Crown of Sargon ended atop the cloud-wreathed ziggurat with the party battered but victorious. Gizem Faruki clung to the black dragon's brow, hammering it with stunning strikes; Miray Ruba Mata Biru and Jean-Zera Mongé, both dropped by its acid breath, dragged themselves back into the fight with moonbeam and mockery. The turning point, though, was the crown itself. Sidra al-Najjar set it upon her brow in the thick of the fray, and the artifact answered with two rebounds: first a fog of memory that left her, Memento-like, unable to recall how she had come to be fighting a dragon at all; then something far greater. The djinn that had lived inside her for as long as she could remember was wrenched bodily out of her, taking shape at her back as a being unto itself. Newly attuned to the crown and steadied by its authority, Sidra spoke a single word of command, and the dragon — down to its last breath — turned and fled into the clouds. The crown was theirs, and the tower let them go.

The change in Sidra ran deeper than any wound. Where the djinn had once worn her like a hermit-crab's shell, now she held it, in its own words, as one holds a leash. It gave her a name at last — Feravi, "the imagined" — and confessed itself grown fond of her over their years together, though it would promise only not to harm her, leaving the question of her friends conspicuously unanswered.

Resting afterward at Osman Hamdi Bey's house, the company turned to the meaning of their shared dreams. Poring over the visions, Jean-Zera Mongé pieced it together: the dreams were no gift and no warning, but a rebound — a psychic bleed-over caught when the four of them had first passed near Mira's home by the archaeological dig, where the diviner's magic and her sister Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun's were locked in a struggle carried in their shared blood. Whether the visions were true events, or Bergüzar's own coloured memories, or something further removed still, none of them could say.

While they made ready to sail — Miray Ruba Mata Biru working the bathhouses for palace gossip, Gizem Faruki passing a Sufi woman's appeal up toward the Harem, Jean-Zera Mongé reading Osman's diary and confirming the dig had only ever been a pretext to find Mira — a last and darkest dream came the night before they left. They looked out again over the Bosphorus, but on an ashen, ruined future: foreign ships on the horizon, aircraft in a grey sky, all of it bearing ill. Bergüzar rushed to the throne room, where her own son sat crowned in his father's place, and begged him, "Don't do this." He only smiled and began to say, "I wish—" and the dreamers woke in a cold sweat to an after-image of the sea rising to swallow the ships, the city cracking open, and darkness falling over all. It was, the mages among them knew, the working of a Wish — magic at its very uttermost.

They crossed by ship in a day and a half and struck out overland toward Mira's farmhouse. But they were not the only ones bound for her door. In the trees near the old dig site, riflemen of the Bostanji Imperial Guard opened fire, and Sidra al-Najjar's new-found sight into minds named their leader: Aziz Sefa Bey, come to kill Mira before the party could reach her. Gizem caught bullets from the air and flung them back, rode down the shooters, and knocked their heads together; Miray's moonbeam, Jean-Zera's whispers, and Sidra's evil eye did the rest. Shaken and outmatched, Aziz worked a ring into a cloud of fog and vanished, and his surviving soldiers stood down. The way to Mira lay open — and the race for her was now in the open too.

The Beats

The Crown Claimed - Atop the ziggurat, the party finished the fight for the Crown of Sargon. When Sidra al-Najjar attuned to the crown mid-battle it cost her a bout of memory loss and then tore her djinn bodily out of her, remaking her; newly crowned, she commanded the black dragon to flee, and the tower released them.

The last of the dragon - The debuffed black dragon fought on, dropping party members with acid breath, while Gizem Faruki rode it and struck at its eyes.

  • Death saves and re-engagement - Miray Ruba Mata Biru and Jean-Zera Mongé, both dropped by the dragon's acid breath, made their death saves and dragged themselves back into the fight.
  • Gizem rides the dragon - Gizem Faruki clung to the black dragon forty feet up, climbing to the back of its head and then to its eyes at the risk of a bite, landing stunning strikes and flurries as it strafed the summit.
  • Miray's ancient curses - Reviving, Miray Ruba Mata Biru flung her oldest and most obscure curses at the dragon as dissonant whispers—insults that had lost their bite in the modern age, hurled all at once as a blast of arcane noise—while Jean-Zera Mongé struck from the ground with chill touch.
  • Hellish rebuke and legendary resistance - Sidra al-Najjar answered the dragon's bite with a hellish rebuke, forcing it to spend the single legendary resistance its diminished state had left it.

The crown and the freed djinn - Sidra al-Najjar set the Crown of Sargon on her brow mid-fight, and its rebounds unmade and then remade her.

  • Memory lost to the crown - Attuning to the Crown of Sargon in battle demanded a hard arcane check, and the miss triggered a rebound: a thirteen-hour short-term memory loss that left Sidra al-Najjar unable to recall—as though waking mid-scene—how she had come to be fighting a dragon at all.
  • Supporting the fallen - Even so disoriented, Sidra al-Najjar read the field, saw Miray Ruba Mata Biru down, and spent a third-level healing to raise her and steady the line.
  • The djinn torn loose - A second attunement, aided by the djinn itself, wrenched Sidra al-Najjar's djinn bodily out of her—where it had lived as long as she could remember—like a spirit punched free of a body; it took physical shape at her back, kind-faced and larger than life, and its first act was fury on her behalf at the dragon.
  • Sorcerer to warlock - The severing remade Sidra al-Najjar from a djinn-possessed sorcerer into a djinn-master warlock, her spell list broadening (command, bane, augury, speak with dead, bestow curse) and her body no longer prey to the monthly "mortal shell" decay it had suffered.
  • Beacon and the dragon commanded - Sidra al-Najjar raised a beacon of hope over her crew, then, steadied by the crown's authority, commanded the black dragon—down to nine hit points—first to stop, and then to flee; it settled, then broke off and flew into the clouds, and she used the crown to sense and open the way out of the tower.

Aftermath and Omens - Resting at Osman Hamdi Bey's house, the party reckoned with Sidra's changed bond to her djinn, traced the source of their shared dreams to Mira, gathered what they could of palace affairs, and shared a final, apocalyptic vision on the eve of departure.

The djinn's new leash - Freed from Sidra's body, the djinn described the new terms of their bond and named itself.

  • Feravi named and leashed - Sidra al-Najjar's djinn took a humanoid form and gave its name as Feravi, "the imagined"; where it had once worn her like a hermit-crab's shell, it now allowed that she held it as one holds a leash, professing fondness grown over their years together but promising only not to harm her—leaving her friends conspicuously unmentioned.
  • The warlock's new powers - As a djinn-master warlock Sidra al-Najjar gained arcane proficiency and deeper knowledge of djinn-kind, the ability to yield her body to Feravi for a burst of force-laden attacks, and an unfathomable nature that turns aside enchantment and divination cast against her; should she fail to hold the leash, Feravi may seek to repossess her.

The dreams explained - Jean-Zera Mongé worked out where the shared visions had come from and what they might be worth.

  • Dreams traced to Mira - Aided by enhance ability, Jean-Zera Mongé determined that the party's shared dreams were a spell rebound caught when they first passed near Mira's home by the archaeological dig—a psychic bleed-over from the blood-borne struggle between the diviner and her sister Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun.
  • Uncertain provenance - The party could not tell whether the visions were true events, Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun's own coloured impressions, or something further removed—how many "levels of inception" down remained unclear.

Preparations and palace intelligence - The company readied a voyage to Mira while probing the affairs of the palace and the Sultan.

  • Bathhouse gossip on an heir - Miray Ruba Mata Biru worked her old bathhouse contacts and picked up palace servants' guarded excitement over a pregnancy and a possible heir "not a blood relation" to them—pieces enough to sense a coming child tied to the palace, unspoken but keenly watched.
  • The Padishah's leanings - The talk placed Padishah Abd-ul Mejid as leaning toward openness—to magic, to non-humans, to modernization, with an eye to a greater Ottoman Empire—though he had not solidified a position, while traditionalists warned against becoming "too much like England or France" and Aziz's faction pressed its own line.
  • A message toward the Harem - Gizem Faruki arranged, through the palace kitchens, for a single written appeal to be relayed to the head of the Harem—framed as a Sufi women's plea for protection and a question of where the Sultan stood on those who practise magic.
  • Osman's diary read - Jean-Zera Mongé read Osman Hamdi Bey's diary at his library and confirmed the archaeological dig had been almost entirely a pretext; Osman's true purpose had been to find Mira, whose existence he had pieced together from fragments the tight-lipped Bergüzar let slip, never naming her sister directly.

The vision of the Wish - The night before sailing, a final dream showed the party a ruined future and the terrible magic that would make it.

  • An apocalyptic future - The dreamers looked out again over the Bosphorus, but onto an ashen, ruined age—the buildings changed, no longer the nineteenth century—Sidra al-Najjar marking foreign ships on the horizon and Gizem Faruki aircraft in a grey sky, all of it bearing ill.
  • Bergüzar's son on the throne - Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun rushed to the throne room to find her own crowned son in his father's place; she begged him, "Don't do this," and he only smiled and began, "I wish—".
  • The Wish and its aftermath - Sidra al-Najjar and Jean-Zera Mongé recognised a Wish spell, magic at its uttermost; the dreamers woke in cold sweat to an after-image of the sea rising to swallow the ships, the city cracking open, the aircraft plucked from the sky, and complete darkness falling over all.

The Ambush on the Road - Crossing by ship and striking overland toward Mira's farmhouse, the party was ambushed by riflemen of the Bostanji Imperial Guard led by Aziz Sefa Bey, who had come to kill Mira first; they drove the ambush off and Aziz fled.

The crossing and the approach - The company took the swifter passage and set out for Mira, aware the dig site and the farmhouse lay slightly apart.

  • Passage by ship - The party booked passage and crossed in a day and a half—Sidra's memory restored on the way—landing near the archaeological dig with the journal's directions to Mira's house to the north-east.
  • Uneasy on the road - Jean-Zera Mongé navigated overland; approaching a clearing near the dig, Gizem Faruki marked a conspicuous absence of birdsong and spotted a rifleman in a tree with his weapon already trained on Jean-Zera.

The fight in the trees - Gunfire opened the ambush; the party named their attacker's leader and broke the assault.

  • The first volley - The hidden shooter's fire struck Jean-Zera Mongé and Gizem Faruki before they could react, while Sidra al-Najjar was warded by a shield of faith and her djinn's nudge; Jean-Zera Mongé's Shield came a beat too late to stop the round.
  • Gizem turns the guns - Gizem Faruki caught a bullet from the air and flung it back with lethal force, nearly knocking its shooter from his tree—pure pre-conscious, divine instinct that carried a lasting favour ("Allah's favour") through the rest of the fight—then ran the prone gunman down.
  • Aziz revealed by detect thoughts - Reading minds through the Crown of Sargon, Sidra al-Najjar found the ambush's calm, unhurried leader expecting his men to finish the party, and probing deeper named him: Aziz Sefa Bey, head of a certain community—on his way to kill Mira—and messaged the crew.
  • Aziz walks in - Aziz Sefa Bey strolled into the firefight with cane and monocle and put two shots at Jean-Zera Mongé's feet, not even trying to kill—"I would have thought you would have learned from our last meeting"—answered by Jean-Zera's "it's good that I'm a slow learner"; his shooters were revealed as elite Bostanji Imperial Guard and a secret-service escort at his side.
  • The birds refuse - Miray Ruba Mata Biru cast speak with animals and called the frightened birds to swarm the ambushers, but they only scattered from the danger; she took to cover and answered Aziz with unsettling words and dissonant whispers.
  • Gizem breaks the line - Gizem Faruki knocked out and disarmed the prone gunman (kicking his rifle into the trees), then rode up on the guardsmen and, with a critical stunning strike, knocked two of them senseless head-to-head; Jean-Zera Mongé's rebukes and whispers and Sidra al-Najjar's hellish rebuke, evil eye, and moonbeam downed several more, including a stalker who had crept in under magical invisibility.
  • Aziz escapes in fog - Shaken and outmatched, Aziz Sefa Bey worked a ring into a fog like a cloud and vanished; with their leader gone and two of them down, the surviving guardsmen stood down and let the party pass, leaving the road to Mira open—and the race for her now in the open.

Moments

  • Memento on the Ziggurat: Sidra al-Najjar set the Crown of Sargon on her head in the middle of the dragon-fight, and the world fell out from under her memory. Like the man in the film who cannot recall why he is standing in the diner, she found herself looking at a downed Miray Ruba Mata Biru and a black dragon banking toward her with no notion of how she had come to be there at all — only that, apparently, she got into shenanigans.
  • The Djinn Torn Loose: The crown's second rebound did what nothing ever had: it pulled the djinn out of her. It felt, Sidra al-Najjar said, like a punch that sends the spirit flying out the other side — a sudden cold nakedness where a companion of years had been — and then there it stood at her back, larger than life, kind-faced, a being at last unto itself. Its first act was fury on her behalf at the dragon that had hurt her.
  • You Showed Him Who's Boss: Newly crowned and steadied, Sidra al-Najjar simply told the dragon to stop, and it did — folding its wings and settling before them with a ferocious, baffled look. A word more and it fled into the clouds with nine hit points to its name. "You survived and thrived and took the crown and attuned to it," said the Keeper, "and didn't even kill the dragon — which, to me, is a superior victory."
  • A Home, and Then a Leash: Asked what had changed between them, the freed djinn found the words. "You were a home that I had made," it told Sidra al-Najjar, "in the way you have seen those crabs that take on a shell. You were a friend — and now you are holding a leash." It had grown fond of her, it said, and meant her no harm. That it said nothing at all about her friends did not go unnoticed.
  • Feravi, the Imagined: The djinn gave its name for the first time — Feravi, "the imagined" — and took the shape, more or less, of a larger-than-life man with a kind face and a shirt left a little too open. It was strange, it allowed, but not distressing. "We may still get up to some mischief, however."
  • The Wrong Place at the Wrong Time: Studying the dreams, Jean-Zera Mongé finally understood: they were nobody's message. When the four of them had first passed the estate by the dig, they had walked through the backwash of a fight — the diviner Mira and her sister Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun straining against each other through their shared blood — and caught the psychic spillover full in the face. How many levels removed from the truth the visions were, no one could say.
  • "I Wish—": The last dream showed a grey and broken future — foreign ships and aircraft in an ashen sky — and Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun running to a throne room where her own son sat crowned. "Don't do this," she begged him; he only smiled and said, "I wish—," and the dreamers woke drenched in cold sweat to an after-image of the sea rising to swallow the fleets, the city splitting open, and darkness taking everything.
  • No Birds: Something was wrong on the road to Mira before anyone drew a weapon. Miray Ruba Mata Biru, used to the noise of the city, marked the silence first — the conspicuous absence of any birdsong — and then, in a low branch, a man with a rifle already sighted on Jean-Zera Mongé.
  • The Mind Behind the Guns: With bullets cracking from the trees, Sidra al-Najjar reached into the leader's thoughts through the crown and found him calm, unhurried, mildly surprised they had come this far — and certain his men would finish them. Then she found the rest: this was Aziz Sefa Bey, and he was on his way to kill Mira.
  • Right Enchilada: A bullet meant for Gizem Faruki never landed — she caught it from the air, spun it, and flung it back hard enough to nearly knock its shooter out of his tree, all of it pure pre-conscious instinct. Muttering her "right enchilada" under her breath, she carried a divine favour through the rest of the fight, catching rounds and knocking guardsmen's heads together like something out of an adventure serial.
  • A Slow Learner: Aziz Sefa Bey walked calmly into the middle of the firefight with his cane and monocle and put two shots at Jean-Zera Mongé's feet — not even trying to kill. "I would have thought you would have learned from our last meeting," he said. "It's good that I'm a slow learner," Jean-Zera answered — and by the end it was Aziz, shaken and outmatched, who worked a ring into a cloud of fog and disappeared, leaving the road to Mira open at last.
