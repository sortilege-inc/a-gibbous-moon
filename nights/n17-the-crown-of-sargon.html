Night 17 · Interlude · 2026-06-14

The Crown of Sargon

Summary

Having talked their way past the guards and through the sewers to reach the heart of the Maiden Tower, the party pressed on toward the Crown of Sargon the cats so badly wanted — and found the tower to be no ordinary keep, but a passage through the ages, each door opening onto a chamber of a vanished civilization and a test of a different virtue. So long as the company met each with curiosity and respect rather than force, no blow was struck against them.

They began in a Babylonian larder, its impossible spread of fruit preserved by a rough lapis-lazuli jewel above an image of Marduk, and were gently ushered out once they had studied it without greed. Beyond lay a Sumerian tomb where two statues woke and spoke: the warrior-king Gilgamesh and the goddess Ishtar, who asked the travellers to recount their feats and journeys. Jean-Zera Mongé obliged with tales of his wanderings while Gizem Faruki answered with a whirling dance, and the pair blessed the company with fortune and compassion — though the audience so pleased Gilgamesh that Jean-Zera could scarcely bear to leave.

The third door opened on the last surviving fragment of the Library of Alexandria, kept by the ghost of an ancient initiate who spoke a hundred tongues. Here the party lingered longest and most gladly: Miray Ruba Mata Biru copied out a druidic spell-scroll under the librarian's hand, Sidra al-Najjar let her djinn read to her from the Septuagint and older texts still, and the whole company took their rest among the shelves, coming away the wiser for it — and a level stronger.

The trials went on. In a felt tent thick with the smell of horses and fermented mare's-milk, three warriors of the steppe tested them at the bow (which Gizem won with a shot that split their champion's arrow), at the cup (which Sidra al-Najjar could not stomach but Miray Ruba Mata Biru met without complaint), and at a contest of power, where a shaman traded spells with Sidra and looked to the heavens for an answer that never came. A Persian council-room set out its little armies to relive the disaster at Ankara, and rewarded the party for the wisdom of leaving old defeats unmeddled with. A voice from a nameless grave posed its riddles — what is greater than the Padishah, what is greater than the sea — until Death itself allowed that only truth and the greater freedom stood above it. A Song-dynasty lord poured tea and asked the hidden secret of the blade, content that they were willing to reason it out. And in a cold prehistoric cave the company laid two of the earliest people gently in their graves, adorning them for whatever came after — and confounding the archaeologists of some far future in the bargain.

At the last, a door opened not onto a room but onto the summit of a vast ziggurat above the clouds, where a crowned skeleton sat in fine cloth with the Crown of Sargon upon its brow — a heavy golden band worked with a crude serpent. Their patience through the trials had not gone unrewarded: when a black dragon dived from the sky to defend the crown, it came stripped of its terror and much of its strength, and the party found themselves immune to its dread. Even so it was a hard fight, its acid breath dropping Miray and Jean-Zera; but Gizem leapt astride it to hammer it with stunning strikes, Miray's moonbeam and the others' magic wore it down, and Sidra took up the crown at last. The prize the cats had named, and the Blue Rider had marked below the Maiden Tower, was theirs.

The Beats

Into the Tower of Ages - Continuing their descent through the Maiden Tower toward the Crown of Sargon, the party passed a serpent-carved door into a hall of paired chambers, each recreating a vanished civilization and testing a different virtue. Meeting each with curiosity rather than force, they were rewarded and ushered onward.

The Babylonian larder - Beyond the carved door the first chamber held an impossible spread of preserved fruit, and the party studied it without greed before being gently pushed back into the hall.

  • A door of clashing styles - Jean-Zera Mongé found the great door an incongruous montage of styles rather than one unified vision; it pushed open easily onto a long hall of paired doors.
  • The preserved larder - The first room offered a lavish spread of fruit and produce kept from rot by a rough but powerful lapis-lazuli jewel suspended above; the food itself was not magical, only preserved.
  • Marduk and the tablets - Inset stone tablets bore cuneiform and an abstract figure of authority the party took for Marduk, patron deity of Babylon; Sidra al-Najjar and Gizem Faruki made rubbings before the room ushered them out.

Gilgamesh and Ishtar - The second chamber was a Sumerian tomb whose two statues woke to question the travellers; answered with stories and a dance, they gave the party their blessings.

  • The waking statues - Two inlaid statues turned to the party and spoke a language the company understood in the mind: the warrior-king Gilgamesh and the goddess Ishtar, who asked to hear of the travellers' great feats and journeys.
  • Tales and a dance - Jean-Zera Mongé recounted his wanderings through Egypt and the Middle East while Gizem Faruki answered with a whirling dance; Sidra al-Najjar's djinn warned that "one of my brethren is furthering" and should be avoided.
  • Blessings of fortune and compassion - Gilgamesh granted a blessing of fortune (a re-rolled attack or save) and Ishtar a blessing of compassion (a bonus to ability checks); pleased overmuch, Gilgamesh's regard briefly bound Jean-Zera Mongé so he would not willingly leave the room, until the door released him.

The Library and the Steppe - The trials continued through the last fragment of the Library of Alexandria, where the party rested and learned, and a steppe camp that tested them at the bow, the cup, and a contest of magic.

The Library of Alexandria - A ghostly librarian kept the surviving remnant of the great library; the party lingered to research, copy, and rest, and grew in power for it.

  • The ancient librarian - A ghostly initiate, speaking a hundred tongues, welcomed the party to what he called the last fragment of the once-great library and bade them study freely but take nothing.
  • Miray copies a scroll - Miray Ruba Mata Biru, scouting in a small shape, met the librarian and, with his help, copied out a druidic spell-scroll from among journals and memoirs of the order.
  • Sidra reads by her djinn - Sidra al-Najjar let her djinn read to her from the Septuagint, the works of Hermes Trismegistus, and early Qur'anic texts, gleaning history and calm from the shelves.
  • Rest and a level gained - Finding the library safe and unhurried, the party rested and researched among the shelves and came away a level stronger.

The steppe warriors' tests - A felt tent of the steppe set three contests before the company—archery, drink, and a display of power—which they met with skill and openness.

  • The archery contest - A steppe warrior challenged the party at the bow; Gizem Faruki, handed a short bow in place of her crossbow, bested him three shots to his, one arrow splitting his own dead-centre.
  • The cup of airag - A second warrior offered fermented mare's-milk; Sidra al-Najjar could not keep it down, but Miray Ruba Mata Biru met the cup without complaint.
  • A trade of magic and a question - A shaman traded spells with Sidra al-Najjar and matched her flame for flame, then bade her work a divination; she asked how to know the right path with her djinn and received quiet validation, while the shaman's own question to the sky went unanswered. The steppe brotherhood honoured the party and opened the way.

Restraint, Riddles, and the Blade - Deeper chambers tested the party's judgement rather than their strength: a Persian war-council they were wise not to meddle with, a grave that answered riddles, a Song-dynasty dialogue on the nature of the blade, and a prehistoric cave that asked reverence for the dead.

The council and the grave - A war-council relived an old defeat, and a nameless grave posed its riddles; the party passed both by restraint and by answering true.

  • The Persian war-council - An opulent chamber of miniature armies replayed the Battle of Ankara (1402), disembodied generals debating in Persian; recognising it as memory, the party chose not to intervene and were released with the tower's approval.
  • The riddles of the grave - A feeble voice from a tomb asked what was greater than the Padishah (Allah, then Death—"Death has conquered even I") and what was greater than the sea; when it asked truth or freedom, Miray Ruba Mata Biru answered "freedom," and the voice named the greater freedom higher even than the throne.

The blade and the dead - A Song-dynasty lord and a prehistoric cave closed out the trials, rewarding the party's willingness to reason and to honour the dead.

  • The secret of the blade - A Song-dynasty warrior poured tea and asked the hidden secret of the blade; the party reasoned over the way of the sword and the blade of grass—that a tool is neutral and made meaningful in the hand—and the lord, adding a line of calligraphy ("serve the country with utmost loyalty"), was satisfied by their willingness to discuss.
  • A burial in the cave - In a cold cave hung with ochre handprints, the party found two of the earliest people dead beside two open graves; Gizem Faruki, Jean-Zera Mongé, and the others washed and buried them with coins and adornments, honouring the dead and confounding some future archaeologist.

The Summit and the Dragon - The last door opened on the summit of a great ziggurat above the clouds, where the Crown of Sargon rested upon a crowned skeleton. A black dragon dived to defend it—stripped of its terror and much of its strength by the party's success in the trials—and after a hard fight the company brought it down and claimed the crown.

The crowned skeleton - The final space was the cloud-wreathed peak of a vast ziggurat, where the prize of the tower sat waiting on the bones of a long-dead king.

  • The peak of the ziggurat - Stepping through the last door, the party found themselves atop a ziggurat above the clouds, the air thin, a crowned skeleton in fine cloth seated before them.
  • The Crown of Sargon - Upon the skeleton's brow sat the Crown of Sargon—a heavy golden band worked with a crude serpent motif, plainly and strongly magical—the prize the cats had sought and the Blue Rider had marked "below the Maiden Tower."

Battle for the crown - A weakened black dragon defended the crown; immune to its dread and aided by the tower's blessings, the party fought it down and took the prize.

  • The dragon's guardianship - A black dragon dived to defend the crown; the party's success in the trials had left them immune to its frightful presence and the dragon itself much diminished in strength.
  • A hard exchange - The dragon's acid breath dropped Miray Ruba Mata Biru and Jean-Zera Mongé; Gizem Faruki leapt astride it to land stunning strikes and flurries, Miray Ruba Mata Biru's moonbeam and Jean-Zera Mongé's mockery and dissonant whispers wore it down, and Sidra al-Najjar burdened it with an evil-eye exhaustion while healing the fallen.
  • The crown claimed - Sidra al-Najjar took up the Crown of Sargon as the party overcame the dragon, ending the trial of the Maiden Tower with the prize in hand.

Moments

  • A Larder Out of Time: The first door opened on an impossible feast—figs and pomegranates and citrus straight from the branch, kept from rot by a rough lapis-lazuli jewel hung above an image of Marduk. It smelled, Jean-Zera Mongé thought, like a master perfumer's blend, and it whispered that this would be a fine place to lie down and sleep. The party made their rubbings, ate nothing, and were quietly pushed back out into the hall.
  • Tell Me of Your Great Feats: In the second chamber two statues turned their heads and spoke in a tongue the party understood in the mind. "Travellers, tell me of your great sports," said Gilgamesh; "you have been on long dreams—tell me, where have you been?" said Ishtar. Jean-Zera Mongé answered with the story of his wanderings, and Gizem Faruki with a dance, and the dead king was so delighted he could hardly let Jean-Zera go.
  • The Last Fragment: The librarian was a ghost who had spoken every language before the party's own and greeted them in each, one after another, until he found one they knew. "This is the last fragment of a once much greater collection," he told them, "all I was able to preserve"—and then bade them read as long as they liked, and take nothing away but copies.
  • Library Time: For once nothing in a room was trying to kill them, and the company could not tear themselves away. Miray Ruba Mata Biru bent over a druidic scroll under the librarian's patient hand; Sidra al-Najjar listened to her djinn read the old scriptures aloud in her mind; and all four rested among the shelves, the small hours of a good all-nighter, everyone locked in.
  • Splitting the Arrow: Handed a short bow in place of her crossbow, Gizem Faruki answered the steppe champion's three shots with three of her own—and one arrow drove clean into the fletching of his, dead centre, Robin Hood's own trick. "Come," said the warrior, honoured, "sit. You are amongst fellow archers."
  • Not for the Faint of Heart: The second warrior poured a cup of fermented mare's-milk and asked who would drink. Sidra al-Najjar tried, and her stomach tried harder; the sight of it coming back up ended her contest. Miray Ruba Mata Biru the druid took her cup as though it were only a wild sort of drink—new, but not strange. "It is not for the faint of heart," laughed the warrior. "You will get used to it in time."
  • An Answer for One, Not the Other: Bidden to show her power, Sidra al-Najjar worked a divination and asked, wordlessly, how she might know the right path with her djinn—and felt only warmth and relief, a sense that she was doing right. The old shaman spoke his own question to the opening sky and waited, and waited, and finally shook his head. "It appears you have received an answer," he said, "and I have not."
  • Leave the Old Wars Be: The Persian council-room laid out its little armies to fight the Battle of Ankara once more, four centuries dead. There was a pull to reach in and change it—to save the home that fell—but the party understood: these were only memories, and the test was whether they would meddle. They let the old defeat lie, and the door opened.
  • Greater Than the Sea: The voice in the grave asked what was greater than the Padishah, and heard: God; and Death—"Death has conquered even I." Then it asked what was greater than the sea, and one of the company answered "truth," to a begrudging respect in the voice. Asked at last to choose between truth and freedom, Miray Ruba Mata Biru chose freedom—and was told the greater freedom stands higher even than the throne.
  • The Blade of Grass and the Sword: The Song lord asked the hidden secret of the blade. Jean-Zera Mongé laid his little-used sword upon the table and set beside it his staff, transformed into a living branch, and let the two speak for themselves; the others reasoned that a blade is neutral, made cruel or kind in the hand that holds it. Satisfied, the lord added six characters of his own—his name, and his mother's charge upon his back: "serve the country with utmost loyalty."
  • Confounding the Archaeologists: In the prehistoric cave the party found two of the earliest people dead by their unfinished graves. Not knowing their rites, Gizem Faruki and Jean-Zera Mongé washed them with snow, laid them out to look powerful and ready for what came after, and set beautiful stones and future coins in their hands—silver on the eyes, one beneath each toe—leaving some archaeologist of ages hence a very confusing dig indeed.
  • The Crown on the Cloud-Top: The last door was no door at all but the summit of a ziggurat above the clouds, the air gone thin, a crowned skeleton in fine cloth seated at the peak. On its brow sat the Crown of Sargon, a heavy golden band worked with a crude serpent—and then a black dragon fell out of the sky to keep it. Their patience had cost the dragon its terror and most of its strength; even so, it took a mounted Gizem Faruki, a chorus of spells, and Sidra's steady hands to bring it down and lift the crown at last.
