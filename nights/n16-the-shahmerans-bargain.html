Night 16 · Interlude · 2026-05-16

The Shahmeran's Bargain

Summary

Deep in the prison beneath the Basilica Cistern, the party's mistake caught up with them at once: the red-dressed prisoner they had freed was no wronged captive but a grotesque sorceress wearing an illusion of beauty. Jean-Zera Mongé glimpsed the truth behind her glamour and, before she could bolt, commanded her to surrender Osman Hamdi Bey's letter. When he moved to shove her, she turned him into a goat. What followed was a chaotic melee — Gizem Faruki grappling and stunning the shrieking hag, Miray Ruba Mata Biru chasing her with a five-foot Moonbeam, Sidra al-Najjar hurling sacred flame — as the sorceress lashed out with a death-glare that dropped Gizem and Sidra where they stood. In the end it was the goat that finished her: Jean-Zera, still transformed, charged and rammed her flat, and the party put her down. She proved to be no one at all — a common sorceress in a cell that had never truly held her, offering neither loot nor answers.

With Jean-Zera stuck in goat-shape for the better part of an hour, the exhausted company barricaded themselves in the empty cell and took a fitful rest, their sleep coloured by the magic still seeping through the walls. They shared a vivid dream of the Topkapi Palace harem: a younger Padishah Abd-ul Mejid watching from a balcony, servants bearing hookah whose smoke drew glowing charms beneath the dreamers' skin, and among them the Eye of Solomon — a symbol at the heart of a magical pact. An older instructress schooled a circle of young women to master their anger and wield their words, invoking Hurrem and Kösem before them, and announced that by order of Padishah Mahmud II they would learn to gaze upon the future with "eyes spying." When the teacher called the name "Bergüzar," the dreamers understood: they had relived the making of the Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun herself, the Padishah's diviner-wife.

Waking, the party turned to the one door they had left unopened: a massive, foreign-wrought portal ringed with chains and locks, projecting the strongest anti-magic field in the prison — and leaking a low thrum of power despite it. Petrus Gyllius's diary supplied the history: in the time of Emperor Justinian and Queen Theodora, the emperor's own magi had subdued a she-devil and sealed her beneath the city. In the neighbouring cell, a burly, snake-eyed grand vizier from the age of Mehmed the Conqueror confirmed it — behind the great door lay "the Shamarin," a monstrous serpent-woman.

Opening the door proved its own ordeal. When Jean-Zera took up the cursed keyring, the curse took him: he became convinced he was Manuel, a Byzantine cataphract sworn to guard the prisoners, and would suffer none to be released. Only by producing Osman's sealed letter — and the mark of the Loremaster of the Lodge of Byzantia — did the party coax the deluded guardian into opening the door not to free the prisoner, but to parley with her.

Beyond it, chained taut from four corners in a wash of pale green light, reared the true The Shahmeran — a serpent-woman twelve feet tall. She had known Osman in life and spoke of him in the past tense, and she offered her aid in exchange for her freedom. The negotiation was fraught: she plunged the chamber into magical darkness, turned Sidra into a lizard, and turned aside every spell the party threw with contemptuous ease, each countered casting rebounding on its caster. Yet she spoke, too, of Constantinople — of protecting all its people, human and magic-touched alike, against the heirs of Justinian — and Sidra al-Najjar's djinn allowed that she was mighty indeed, though "there are always repercussions to her actions."

She asked only that a single chain be broken. Miray, in the shape of a giant crab, and Sidra between them severed two, and the Shahmeran counted the bargain kept. She took up Osman's Journal, and where the party and every scholar before them had failed, she unravelled its cipher — a rewriting of the very rules of communication, built on the secret magic of the Lodge. The journal's purpose, she read aloud, was to hide Mira — Bergüzar's sister and a diviner whose living presence smothers the Haseki Sultan's full power, the effect swelling as Mira draws nearer. Osman had kept Mira alive but distant, concealed her whereabouts in the journal, and erased even his own memory of them. Now the party knew: Mira could be found on a farm near the archaeological dig at Thymbra Farm — and they alone would decide whether to deliver her to the Padishah's cause or shield her from it.

They spent a scroll of Greater Restoration to lift the cataphract's curse from Jean-Zera, restoring him to his own mind and body, and turned at last toward the surface — and toward Mira.

They did not go straight for her. The cats' prize — the Crown of Sargon, marked below the Maiden Tower — still waited, and the company turned there first. They talked their way past the tower's mechanical guardians, whose gaze could have turned them to stone, left the puzzled wardens a pair of magical spectacles, and pieced together the tower's old legend: it had once caged a maiden imprisoned by her own father, the emperor Constantine, her fate entangled with a serpent. A little further on they came to a great door carved over with coiling snakes — and there, for the night, they stopped. (This last stretch survives only as the following session's recap, and is recorded here in brief.)

The Beats

The Sorceress Unmasked - Picking up where they had left off in the prison beneath the Basilica Cistern, the party found that the red-dressed prisoner they had freed was no wronged captive but a grotesque sorceress cloaked in an illusion of beauty. Over a running fight she surrendered Osman Hamdi Bey's letter, turned Jean-Zera Mongé into a goat, and struck several of the party to the ground with a death-inducing glare before they killed her; she left no loot and her cell showed no sign of ever having held her.

Illusion pierced and letter seized - Only Jean-Zera Mongé saw the hag behind the glamour; the party curses her, commands her, and provokes her before she can flee.

  • Jean-Zera sees the true form - Jean-Zera Mongé's tell for illusion is a reverse-synaesthesia "buzz at the edge of hearing"; making his save, he chose to see the sorceress's true, grotesque form while Gizem Faruki, Sidra al-Najjar, and Miray Ruba Mata Biru still saw a beautiful woman in her mid-thirties in a red dress with red hair and a pearl pendant.
  • Sidra's Nazar bead - Sidra al-Najjar discreetly crushed a Nazar bead and blew its dust at the sorceress, imposing disadvantage on her next check or attack.
  • The Command for the letter - Jean-Zera Mongé swapped a prepared spell for Command and, pointing at the letter, spoke "give me"; unable to resist, the sorceress handed over Osman Hamdi Bey's sealed letter.
  • A shove answered with a goat - When Jean-Zera Mongé moved to shove her, the sorceress used her reaction to transform him into a goat, then tried to shove past the party and run for the door.

The goat, the grapple, and the glare - As the sorceress ran, the party pinned her and burned her down while she frightened them with her true shape and dropped several to zero with a death-glare.

  • Gizem grapples and is frightened - Gizem Faruki lunged and grappled the fleeing sorceress and landed a stunning strike; catching sight of her true form as she did, Gizem failed a save and was frightened, clenching the shrieking hag in terror without daring to strike her.
  • Miray's readied Moonbeam - Miray Ruba Mata Biru had readied Moonbeam and triggered its five-foot beam on the doorway as the sorceress broke for it, holding it to follow her; a deception check kept the sorceress from realising Miray was the caster.
  • Sacred flame and blissful ignorance - Sidra al-Najjar hurled sacred flame (2d8 as the hag failed her save) but then failed her own perception—ironically sparing her the sight of the hag through the illusion, so she fought on still seeing a beautiful woman.
  • The cold, clammy skin - Compelled by fear to release her grapple, Gizem Faruki recoiled on realising the sorceress's skin was cold, clammy, and damp, then flailed a disadvantaged swing that missed twice.
  • The goat's vicious mockery - Still a goat, Jean-Zera Mongé worked his unfamiliar mouth around a verbal component (a dexterity check) and bleated out a vicious mockery; the sorceress saved but the intent was plain, and she was not intimidated by a goat.
  • The death-glare - The sorceress proved no shapechanger but a raw spellcaster with a glare that brought a failed target straight to zero; over the fight it dropped Gizem Faruki (who first resisted it) and Sidra al-Najjar, while Moonbeam ground her down.
  • The goat rams her down - Charging twenty feet, Jean-Zera Mongé the goat rammed the sorceress, knocking her prone as she failed her strength save, and the party finished her at two hit points.

A hollow victory - Searching the dead sorceress and her cell, the party found nothing and understood they had freed and killed a nobody.

  • No prize on the body - The sorceress carried nothing of value—only rags that had aged though her body had not.
  • The cell that never held her - Sidra al-Najjar noted the cell had no feeding-slot, no bedding, no privy, and no residue of magic or evil; it looked suspiciously clean and empty, as though never truly occupied—an "all-risk, no-reward" fight.
  • Jean-Zera stuck as a goat - The party learned the goat-shape would hold for roughly forty minutes and that they had no ready means to reverse it.

Rest and the Making of Bergüzar - Waiting out Jean-Zera's transformation, the party barricaded the empty cell and took a poor rest amid the leaking magic, sharing a dream in which they relived the training and empowerment of the young Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun in the palace harem.

A fitful long rest - The company sealed themselves in and rested badly.

  • Barricaded to rest - Judging the cell as safe a hiding place as any—no guard, Bichura, Medusa, or Merrow was likely to follow—the party barricaded it and took a long rest.
  • A rest less than restful - Constitution saves at disadvantage left the rest imperfect, the sleepers steeped in the magic bleeding through the walls.

The dream of the harem - The dreamers inhabited a young woman of the harem as a diviner's gift was awakened by a pact.

  • The harem and the hookah - Sidra al-Najjar recognised the harem section of the Topkapi Palace; a younger Padishah Abd-ul Mejid watched from a balcony as servants brought hookah whose smoke drew glowing, henna-like charms beneath the dreamers' skin.
  • The Eye of Solomon - Among the charms an eye appeared, which the dreamers recognised as the Eye of Solomon—a symbol at the heart of a magical pact then being sealed, its terms unknown to them.
  • The instructress's lesson - An older teacher schooled a circle of young women to master their anger and wield their words—"it is your words that hold the most power"—invoking Hurrem Sultan and Kösem Sultan, and declaring that by order of Padishah Mahmud II they would learn to gaze upon the world and the future with "eyes spying," as one dreamer's eyes turned white.
  • The name Bergüzar - When the teacher called "Bergüzar," the dreamers understood they inhabited the young Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun herself—the Sultan of their earlier dreams, now named—as she levitated amid rising stones and flowers, the clock-tower's hands spinning, and woke.
  • Magic through the walls - Waking, the party sensed a strong, non-malicious thrum of residual magic bleeding through walls plainly built to contain it, in which they had been steeping as they slept.

The Great Sealed Door - The party turned to the one door they had left shut: a massive, foreign-wrought portal ringed with chains and locks, projecting the prison's strongest anti-magic field yet leaking power. Petrus Gyllius's diary and a snake-eyed grand vizier in the next cell named its prisoner as the Shahmeran, imprisoned since Justinian's day.

The door and its history - The party read the door, the diary, and a neighbouring prisoner to learn what lay behind it.

  • A door of many styles - The reinforced central door was foreign-wrought, an incongruous montage of styles rather than one vision; Jean-Zera Mongé's detect magic read a powerful anti-magic field on it and a magical leakage from within, the source of the thrum the party had felt.
  • Petrus's account - Piecing together Petrus Gyllius's diary (circa 1544) over some ninety minutes, the party learned that under Emperor Justinian and Queen Theodora the emperor's own magi had subdued the Shahmeran and imprisoned her beneath the city.
  • The winch mechanism - Examining the door's edges, the party found a chain running through a winch beside it, apparently a mechanism rather than a trap on the door.
  • The snake-eyed grand vizier - In the neighbouring cell a burly, bearded grand vizier from the age of Mehmed the Conqueror—his eyes yellowed and slitted like a serpent's—awaited release or execution and confirmed the great door held "the Shamarin," a massive serpent-woman, one of "the true devils of the old world," warning the party against her.

Jean-Zera cursed as the cataphract - The cursed keyring took Jean-Zera and remade him into a guardian sworn to keep every prisoner sealed.

  • The keyring's curse - Jean-Zera Mongé's Mage Hand dissipated against the anti-magic door and the cursed keyring dropped; on taking it up he failed his save and became convinced he was Manuel, a Byzantine cataphract and guardian of the prison, gaining great temporary vigour and warded spells but refusing to free any prisoner.
  • Persuading the guardian - The party produced Osman Hamdi Bey's sealed letter and the mark of the Loremaster of the Lodge of Byzantia to convince the deluded Jean-Zera that they came on lawful business—only to speak with the prisoner, not free her—and that opening the door would disable the anti-magic without releasing her.
  • The message to the serpent - Sidra al-Najjar sent a message cantrip toward the door—"a friend of Osman's family, we seek your assistance"—drawing no words but a rumble that shook the walls.
  • The door opened - Jean-Zera opened the great door onto a wash of pale green light, disabling the anti-magic field while runic chains from the four corners held the prisoner taut as a failsafe.

Parley with the Shahmeran - Beyond the door reared the true The Shahmeran—a serpent-woman twelve feet tall, chained from four corners. She offered aid for freedom; after a fraught, magic-laced negotiation the party broke two chains and she decrypted Osman's Journal, revealing the secret of Mira and her location.

A dangerous negotiation - The Shahmeran demanded to be unbound; when the party hesitated she loosed darkness and transformation, and their spells rebounded.

  • The Shahmeran revealed - The great serpent-woman raised herself twelve feet from a vast coil in the pale green light, acknowledged the party's message, and said she would help once unbound; the four runic chains held her active only while the anti-magic field was down.
  • She knew Osman - The The Shahmeran said Osman had visited her before and spoke of him in the past tense, noting his first duty had been to a state that "inherited my bindings," and that he had not been at liberty.
  • Darkness and a lizard - When the party balked, the Shahmeran plunged the chamber into magical darkness—cover, she said, for any who wished to disagree unseen—and turned Sidra al-Najjar into a lizard, countering the party's spells so each rebounded on its caster.
  • Positioning in the dark - Miray Ruba Mata Biru took the shape of a giant crab for blindsight and grappling and worked toward the chains; Gizem Faruki blocked the doorway with see invisibility; a charm turned the cursed "Manuel" to wander into the dark.
  • The djinn's warning - Sidra al-Najjar consulted her djinn, who knew the Shahmeran only by reputation—exceptionally adept with magic, but "there are always repercussions to her actions," and she had once "fought and lost."

The debate and the chains - The party argued her cause, weighed the risk, and broke the chains she required.

  • Her case for the city - The The Shahmeran pressed her cause: she had brought forth great magic from the earth to defend Constantinople against Justinian's conquest, and sought to protect all its people—human and magic-touched, of every faith—against the heirs of Justinian, who had turned the gift against their own.
  • One chain, and then two - Asking only that a single chain be broken, the Shahmeran watched Miray Ruba Mata Biru the giant crab snap one chain clean and Sidra al-Najjar sever the magical "circuit" of a second; she counted the bargain fulfilled twice over, two of four chains now inert.
  • A rebound in the exchange - When Jean-Zera Mongé cast Spirit Guardians she counterspelled and poached the spell for herself; the rebound left Jean-Zera's maximum vigour reduced by thirteen until mended.

The cipher and the secret of Mira - The Shahmeran unravelled the journal Osman had sealed against every reader, laying bare his errand and the party's coming choice.

  • The journal decrypted - The The Shahmeran took up Osman's Journal, its pages turning at her word, and unpicked its cipher—a Lodge working that "rewrites the fundamental rules of communication," simple as turning oneself to air—reading it where the party and every scholar had failed, for "some of us know the magic the lodge was built upon."
  • Karya, Bergüzar, and Mira - She named "Karya" (a child-name) and "Bergüzar" as one person—the Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun—and confirmed Mira as her sister.
  • The diviner who mutes the Sultan - The journal revealed that Mira's living presence prevents Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun from reaching the full, Theodora-like power of a diviner—the nullifying effect swelling the nearer Mira draws—and that this alone stops the Padishah's grand plan.
  • Osman's concealment - Osman had kept Mira alive but far away, hidden her whereabouts in the journal under mundane and arcane locks, and erased even his own memory of them; the disturbance at the archaeological dig that first drew the party in had been a rebound of Mira's own magic.
  • Mira's location given - The The Shahmeran gave up Mira's location—a farm not far from the archaeological dig at Thymbra Farm—and urged the party to keep the diviner alive, or bring her to the city to check Bergüzar, rather than see another Theodora rise; to reach the Shahmeran again, she said, they need only ask Sidra's djinn.
  • The curse lifted and the ascent - The party spent a scroll of Greater Restoration to free Jean-Zera Mongé from the cataphract's curse—mending his reduced vigour in the bargain—reached level six, and turned toward the surface and toward Mira.

To the Maiden Tower - Surfacing from the prison, the party turned first not to Mira but to the Maiden Tower and the Crown of Sargon the cats had marked below it; they talked their way past the tower's mechanical guardians, slipped a petrifying gaze that could have turned them to stone, learned the old legend of the maiden caged there, and came at last to a great serpent-carved door. Past the guardians - The tower's automaton wardens and their stone-turning gaze were met with words rather than force.

  • The mechanical wardens - The way into the Maiden Tower was held by mechanical guardians whose gaze could turn a living creature to stone; none of the company was petrified, and they talked the wardens down rather than cutting through them.
  • The parting spectacles - Talking past the guardians, the party left them a pair of magical spectacles in the bargain—the wardens puzzling over what the things were even meant for.
  • The tower's captive maiden - The party pieced together the tower's old legend: it had once caged a maiden imprisoned by her own father, the Byzantine emperor (basileus) Emperor Constantine, her keeping bound up with the serpent-dragon Yutba.
  • The serpent-carved door - Descending a little from the wardens' post, the company came to a majestic door worked all over with serpentine carvings—the threshold of the trials to come, where the next night's climb would begin.

Moments

  • The Beauty and the Hag: The red-dressed prisoner still wore her borrowed loveliness for most of the party, but Jean-Zera Mongé chose to see the truth: "the most grotesque looking caricature of a head you've ever seen." Where the fairy tales had always seemed to exaggerate their swamp-witches for effect, here was the thing itself, and no exaggeration at all.
  • "Gimme": With the sorceress clutching Osman Hamdi Bey's letter, Jean-Zera Mongé pointed and spoke a single word of Command—"give me"—and, unable to resist, she pressed the folded letter into his hand before the fight had truly begun.
  • The Buffest Goat That Can Goat: A shove met a spell, and Jean-Zera Mongé found himself on four hooves. Reduced to a goat but not to silence, he worked his unfamiliar mouth around a verbal component and bleated out a vicious mockery; later, charging twenty feet across the cell, he rammed the sorceress flat and finished what the party had started.
  • All Risk, No Reward: When the sorceress finally fell, the party searched her and found nothing—aged rags on a body that had not aged, an empty cell that had never truly been a cell, and no trace of the evil they had feared. "Sadly," the Keeper admitted, "this was an all-risk, no-reward encounter."
  • Eyes Spying: In their shared dream the young women of the harem drew charms of light beneath their skin with every draw of the hookah, and among them shone the Eye of Solomon. "By order of his Imperial Majesty, the Padishah Mahmud II," the instructress declared, "we will gaze upon the world and the future with eyes spying"—and one dreamer's eyes turned white.
  • The Name in the Dream: The teacher called out a name and the dreamers realized, with a lurch, that they were the one being called. "Bergüzar," she said—and the woman whose youthful face had haunted their sleep for weeks finally had a name, and a beginning.
  • The Snake-Eyed Vizier: The burly, bearded man in the next cell had waited so long for release or execution that he still spoke of Mehmed the Conqueror as though he might yet change his mind. When he turned his head, his eyes showed a snake's yellow slit. He named the thing behind the great door without hesitation: "a massive snake person… the Shamarin."
  • I Am the Cataphract: The moment Jean-Zera Mongé closed his hand on the cursed keyring, another man's certainty flooded in. He was Manuel now—a cataphract, guardian of the prison—and he would suffer no prisoner to walk free. His friends found themselves arguing their innocence to a companion who no longer knew their names.
  • The Serpent in Pale Green Light: The great door swung wide on a wash of pale green light, and there she was—coil upon coil, rising twelve feet in the middle of a chamber whose four corners strained to hold her taut. "Unbind me," said the The Shahmeran, "and I will help."
  • Hide and Seek in the Dark: When the party balked, the Shahmeran plunged the room into darkness—cover, she explained, for whichever of them wished to disagree with the others unseen. In that black, Sidra became a lizard, Miray became a giant crab feeling along the chains, and a whispered charm turned one friend against another.
  • The Rubik's Cube: The journal that had defeated every scholar delighted the Shahmeran like a puzzle-box in the hands of someone who had helped design the box. "Some of us know the magic the lodge was built upon," she said, turning the floating pages, unpicking a cipher that "rewrites the fundamental rules of communication"—simple as turning a person into a gust of wind.
  • As Long as Mira Lives: Reading Osman's true errand aloud, the Shahmeran laid the choice bare. Mira's living presence smothered her sister Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun's power, and the nearer she came the more it was muted. Osman had hidden the diviner on a farm near the old dig, erased even his own memory of the place—and now the party alone would decide whether Mira should be delivered to the Padishah, or kept far from him.
  • Past the Stone Gaze: The way into the Maiden Tower was kept by mechanical wardens whose look could turn flesh to stone. The party never drew on them—they talked their way through instead, and left the guardians a pair of magical spectacles on the way past, the automata still puzzling over what they were for.
  • The Maiden and the Serpent: The tower had a story older than the party's errand—a maiden shut away by her own father, the emperor Constantine, her keeping bound up with a serpent. Beyond the guardians a great door waited, carved over with coiling snakes, and there the night's road ended.
