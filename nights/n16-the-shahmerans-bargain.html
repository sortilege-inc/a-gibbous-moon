Night 16 · Interlude · 2026-05-16

The Shahmeran's Bargain

Summary

Deep in the prison beneath the Basilica Cistern, the party's mistake caught up with them at once: the red-dressed prisoner they had freed was no wronged captive but a grotesque sorceress wearing an illusion of beauty. Jean-Zera Mongé glimpsed the truth behind her glamour and, before she could bolt, commanded her to surrender Osman Hamdi Bey's letter. When he moved to shove her, she turned him into a goat. What followed was a chaotic melee — Gizem Faruki grappling and stunning the shrieking hag, Miray Ruba Mata Biru chasing her with a five-foot Moonbeam, Sidra al-Najjar hurling sacred flame — as the sorceress lashed out with a death-glare that dropped Gizem and Sidra where they stood. In the end it was the goat that finished her: Jean-Zera, still transformed, charged and rammed her flat, and the party put her down. She proved to be no one at all — a common sorceress in a cell that had never truly held her, offering neither loot nor answers.

With Jean-Zera stuck in goat-shape for the better part of an hour, the exhausted company barricaded themselves in the empty cell and took a fitful rest, their sleep coloured by the magic still seeping through the walls. They shared a vivid dream of the Topkapi Palace harem: a younger Padishah Abd-ul Mejid watching from a balcony, servants bearing hookah whose smoke drew glowing charms beneath the dreamers' skin, and among them the Eye of Solomon — a symbol at the heart of a magical pact. An older instructress schooled a circle of young women to master their anger and wield their words, invoking Hurrem and Kösem before them, and announced that by order of Padishah Mahmud II they would learn to gaze upon the future with "eyes spying." When the teacher called the name "Bergüzar," the dreamers understood: they had relived the making of the Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun herself, the Padishah's diviner-wife.

Waking, the party turned to the one door they had left unopened: a massive, foreign-wrought portal ringed with chains and locks, projecting the strongest anti-magic field in the prison — and leaking a low thrum of power despite it. Petrus Gyllius's diary supplied the history: in the time of Emperor Justinian and Queen Theodora, the emperor's own magi had subdued a she-devil and sealed her beneath the city. In the neighbouring cell, a burly, snake-eyed grand vizier from the age of Mehmed the Conqueror confirmed it — behind the great door lay "the Shamarin," a monstrous serpent-woman.

Opening the door proved its own ordeal. When Jean-Zera took up the cursed keyring, the curse took him: he became convinced he was Manuel, a Byzantine cataphract sworn to guard the prisoners, and would suffer none to be released. Only by producing Osman's sealed letter — and the mark of the Loremaster of the Lodge of Byzantia — did the party coax the deluded guardian into opening the door not to free the prisoner, but to parley with her.

Beyond it, chained taut from four corners in a wash of pale green light, reared the true The Shahmeran — a serpent-woman twelve feet tall. She had known Osman in life and spoke of him in the past tense, and she offered her aid in exchange for her freedom. The negotiation was fraught: she plunged the chamber into magical darkness, turned Sidra into a lizard, and turned aside every spell the party threw with contemptuous ease, each countered casting rebounding on its caster. Yet she spoke, too, of Constantinople — of protecting all its people, human and magic-touched alike, against the heirs of Justinian — and Sidra al-Najjar's djinn allowed that she was mighty indeed, though "there are always repercussions to her actions."

She asked only that a single chain be broken. Miray, in the shape of a giant crab, and Sidra between them severed two, and the Shahmeran counted the bargain kept. She took up Osman's Journal, and where the party and every scholar before them had failed, she unravelled its cipher — a rewriting of the very rules of communication, built on the secret magic of the Lodge. The journal's purpose, she read aloud, was to hide Mira — Bergüzar's sister and a diviner whose living presence smothers the Haseki Sultan's full power, the effect swelling as Mira draws nearer. Osman had kept Mira alive but distant, concealed her whereabouts in the journal, and erased even his own memory of them. Now the party knew: Mira could be found on a farm near the archaeological dig at Thymbra Farm — and they alone would decide whether to deliver her to the Padishah's cause or shield her from it.

They spent a scroll of Greater Restoration to lift the cataphract's curse from Jean-Zera, restoring him to his own mind and body, and turned at last toward the surface — and toward Mira.

The Beats

The Sorceress Unmasked - Picking up in the prison beneath the Basilica Cistern, the red-dressed prisoner the party had freed proved to be a grotesque sorceress hiding behind an illusion of beauty. She surrendered Osman Hamdi Bey's letter under duress, turned Jean-Zera Mongé into a goat, and fought to escape with a death-inducing glare before the party finally killed her; she left no loot and no answers.

Illusion pierced and letter seized - Jean-Zera Mongé saw through the sorceress's glamour to the hag beneath, and the party moved against her before she could flee her cell.

  • Jean-Zera perceives the illusion - Jean-Zera Mongé detected the sorceress's disguise as a "buzz at the edge of hearing" and chose to see her true, grotesque form; the others still saw a beautiful woman in a red dress.
  • Sidra curses her with a Nazar bead - Sidra al-Najjar discreetly crushed a Nazar bead and blew the dust at the sorceress, imposing disadvantage on her next action.
  • Jean-Zera commands the letter - Jean-Zera Mongé cast Command—"give me"—forcing the sorceress to hand over Osman Hamdi Bey's sealed letter.
  • Shove answered with a curse - When Jean-Zera Mongé tried to shove her, the sorceress transformed him into a goat and bolted for the door.

The goat and the death-glare - As the sorceress ran, the party closed in—Gizem grappling and stunning her, Miray and Sidra burning her from range—while she frightened them with her true form and struck several to zero with a death-glare.

  • Gizem grapples and stuns the hag - Gizem Faruki seized the fleeing sorceress, saw her true form (and was frightened), and landed a stunning strike; the sorceress could not act.
  • Miray chases with Moonbeam - Miray Ruba Mata Biru loosed a five-foot Moonbeam on the doorway and held it to follow the sorceress as she fled, concealing that the caster was Miray.
  • Death-glare drops the party - The sorceress's glare brought Gizem Faruki and Sidra al-Najjar to zero hit points in turn; she was no shapechanger but a raw spellcaster.
  • The goat rams her down - Still transformed, Jean-Zera Mongé managed a goat's ram that knocked the sorceress prone, and the party finished her.
  • No prize in the empty cell - The dead sorceress carried nothing of value, her rags aged though she had not; her cell showed no sign of ever having been occupied, and no magical residue lingered—an "all-risk, no-reward" fight.

Rest and the Making of Bergüzar - Unable to restore Jean-Zera from goat-shape for the better part of an hour, the party barricaded the empty cell and rested badly amid the leaking magic. They shared a dream of the palace harem in which they relived the training and empowerment of the young Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun.

Fitful rest in a leaking prison - The company sealed themselves in the sorceress's cell to wait out Jean-Zera's transformation and take a long rest, though the rest proved less than restful.

  • Jean-Zera trapped as a goat - The party learned the goat-shape would hold for roughly forty minutes and that they had no ready means to reverse it, so they settled in to wait.
  • Barricaded for a long rest - The company judged the cell as safe a hiding place as any—no guard, Bichura, Medusa, or Merrow was likely to follow—and barricaded it to rest.

Shared dream of the harem - The dreamers found themselves in the Topkapi Palace harem, inhabiting a young woman among many, as a magical pact was sealed and a diviner's gift awakened.

  • The harem and the Eye of Solomon - Sidra al-Najjar recognized the harem section of the palace; a younger Padishah Abd-ul Mejid watched from a balcony as hookah-smoke drew glowing charms beneath the dreamers' skin, among them the Eye of Solomon, marking a magical pact.
  • The instructress's lesson - An older teacher schooled the young women to master their anger and wield their words, invoking Hurrem Sultan and Kösem Sultan, and declared that by order of Padishah Mahmud II they would learn to gaze upon the future with "eyes spying."
  • The name Bergüzar revealed - When the teacher called "Bergüzar," the dreamers understood they inhabited the young Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun herself—confirming the Sultan of their earlier dreams—as she levitated amid rising stone and flowers and the clock-tower's hands spun.
  • Magic seeps through the walls - Waking, the party sensed a strong thrum of residual, non-malicious magic bleeding through walls plainly built to contain it.

The Great Sealed Door - Turning to the one door left unopened, the party found a foreign-wrought, chain-bound portal with the prison's strongest anti-magic field—the source of the leaking magic. Petrus Gyllius's diary and a snake-eyed grand vizier next door named its prisoner: the Shahmeran, imprisoned since Justinian's day.

The door and its history - The party examined the reinforced central door and drew on Petrus's diary and a neighbouring prisoner to learn what lay behind it.

  • Petrus's diary on the imprisonment - Petrus Gyllius's diary (circa 1544) recounted how, under Emperor Justinian and Queen Theodora, the emperor's own magi subdued the Shahmeran and imprisoned her beneath the city.
  • A chain-and-winch mechanism - The party noted a chain running through a winch beside the door, apparently a mechanism rather than a trap on the door itself.
  • The snake-eyed grand vizier - In the neighbouring cell a burly, bearded grand vizier from the age of Mehmed the Conqueror—his eyes yellowed and slitted like a serpent's—awaited release or execution and confirmed the great door held "the Shamarin," a monstrous serpent-woman, one of "the true devils of the old world."

Jean-Zera cursed as the cataphract - Taking up the cursed keyring to open the door, Jean-Zera Mongé was overcome by its curse and believed himself a Byzantine guardian sworn to keep every prisoner sealed.

  • The cursed keyring takes Jean-Zera - When Jean-Zera Mongé picked up the cursed keyring, he became convinced he was Manuel, a cataphract and guardian of the prison, gaining great temporary vigour and spells but refusing to release any prisoner.
  • Persuading the deluded guardian - The party produced Osman's sealed letter and the mark of the Loremaster of the Lodge of Byzantia to convince the cursed Jean-Zera that they came on lawful business—not to free the Shahmeran, but to speak with her—and that opening the door need not release her.
  • The door opened - Jean-Zera opened the great door onto a wash of pale green light, disabling the anti-magic field while runic chains from the four corners held the prisoner as a failsafe.

Parley with the Shahmeran - Behind the door reared the true The Shahmeran, chained taut and twelve feet tall. She offered aid in exchange for freedom; after a fraught, magic-laced negotiation the party broke two chains, and she decrypted Osman's Journal, revealing the secret of Mira and her location.

A dangerous negotiation - The Shahmeran demanded to be unbound before she would help; when the party hesitated she loosed darkness and transformation, and every spell they answered with rebounded.

  • The Shahmeran revealed - The great serpent-woman raised herself twelve feet from a vast coil in the pale green light, acknowledged she had received the party's message, and said she would help once unbound.
  • She knew Osman - The The Shahmeran said Osman had visited her before and spoke of him in the past tense, noting his first duty had been to a state that inherited her bindings.
  • Darkness and rebounds - The Shahmeran plunged the chamber into magical darkness, turned Sidra al-Najjar into a lizard, and countered the party's spells so that each rebounded on its caster—Jean-Zera's later casting cost him a lasting reduction to his maximum vigour.
  • The djinn's warning - Sidra al-Najjar consulted her djinn, who knew the Shahmeran only by reputation: exceptionally adept with magic, but "there are always repercussions to her actions," and she had once "fought and lost."

The chains and the cipher - The party weighed the risk, broke the chains she required, and watched her unravel the journal Osman had sealed against every reader.

  • Two chains broken - Miray Ruba Mata Biru, in the shape of a giant crab, and Sidra al-Najjar severed the magic binding two of the four chains; the Shahmeran counted the single chain she had asked for repaid twice over and the bargain fulfilled.
  • The journal decrypted - The The Shahmeran took up Osman's Journal and unravelled its cipher—a Lodge working that "rewrites the fundamental rules of communication," simple as turning oneself to air—reading it where the party and every scholar had failed.
  • Bergüzar and her sister named - The Shahmeran identified "Arcaria" (a child-name) and "Bergüzar" as one person, the Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun, and confirmed Mira as her sister.

The secret of Mira - The decrypted journal laid bare Osman's true errand and the party's coming choice, and the Shahmeran gave up Mira's location before urging them to keep the diviner alive.

  • The diviner who mutes the Sultan - The journal revealed that Mira's living presence prevents Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun from reaching the full, Theodora-like power of a diviner, the nullifying effect swelling the nearer Mira draws; Osman had kept Mira alive but far away, hidden her whereabouts in the journal, and erased his own memory of them.
  • The inciting rebound explained - The Shahmeran read that the disturbance at the archaeological dig which first drew the party in had been a rebound of Mira's own magic.
  • Mira's location given - The Shahmeran gave up Mira's location—a farm not far from the archaeological dig at Thymbra Farm—and urged the party to keep the diviner alive, or bring her to the city to check Bergüzar, rather than see another Theodora rise.
  • The curse lifted and the ascent - The party spent a scroll of Greater Restoration to free Jean-Zera Mongé from the cataphract's curse (also mending his reduced vigour), agreed they could reach the Shahmeran again through Sidra's djinn, and turned toward the surface and toward Mira.

Moments

  • The Beauty and the Hag: The red-dressed prisoner still wore her borrowed loveliness for most of the party, but Jean-Zera Mongé chose to see the truth: "the most grotesque looking caricature of a head you've ever seen." Where the fairy tales had always seemed to exaggerate their swamp-witches for effect, here was the thing itself, and no exaggeration at all.
  • "Gimme": With the sorceress clutching Osman Hamdi Bey's letter, Jean-Zera Mongé pointed and spoke a single word of Command—"give me"—and, unable to resist, she pressed the folded letter into his hand before the fight had truly begun.
  • The Buffest Goat That Can Goat: A shove met a spell, and Jean-Zera Mongé found himself on four hooves. Reduced to a goat but not to silence, he worked his unfamiliar mouth around a verbal component and bleated out a vicious mockery; later, charging twenty feet across the cell, he rammed the sorceress flat and finished what the party had started.
  • All Risk, No Reward: When the sorceress finally fell, the party searched her and found nothing—aged rags on a body that had not aged, an empty cell that had never truly been a cell, and no trace of the evil they had feared. "Sadly," the Keeper admitted, "this was an all-risk, no-reward encounter."
  • Eyes Spying: In their shared dream the young women of the harem drew charms of light beneath their skin with every draw of the hookah, and among them shone the Eye of Solomon. "By order of his Imperial Majesty, the Padishah Mahmud II," the instructress declared, "we will gaze upon the world and the future with eyes spying"—and one dreamer's eyes turned white.
  • The Name in the Dream: The teacher called out a name and the dreamers realized, with a lurch, that they were the one being called. "Bergüzar," she said—and the woman whose youthful face had haunted their sleep for weeks finally had a name, and a beginning.
  • The Snake-Eyed Vizier: The burly, bearded man in the next cell had waited so long for release or execution that he still spoke of Mehmed the Conqueror as though he might yet change his mind. When he turned his head, his eyes showed a snake's yellow slit. He named the thing behind the great door without hesitation: "a massive snake person… the Shamarin."
  • I Am the Cataphract: The moment Jean-Zera Mongé closed his hand on the cursed keyring, another man's certainty flooded in. He was Manuel now—a cataphract, guardian of the prison—and he would suffer no prisoner to walk free. His friends found themselves arguing their innocence to a companion who no longer knew their names.
  • The Serpent in Pale Green Light: The great door swung wide on a wash of pale green light, and there she was—coil upon coil, rising twelve feet in the middle of a chamber whose four corners strained to hold her taut. "Unbind me," said the The Shahmeran, "and I will help."
  • Hide and Seek in the Dark: When the party balked, the Shahmeran plunged the room into darkness—cover, she explained, for whichever of them wished to disagree with the others unseen. In that black, Sidra became a lizard, Miray became a giant crab feeling along the chains, and a whispered charm turned one friend against another.
  • The Rubik's Cube: The journal that had defeated every scholar delighted the Shahmeran like a puzzle-box in the hands of someone who had helped design the box. "Some of us know the magic the lodge was built upon," she said, turning the floating pages, unpicking a cipher that "rewrites the fundamental rules of communication"—simple as turning a person into a gust of wind.
  • As Long as Mira Lives: Reading Osman's true errand aloud, the Shahmeran laid the choice bare. Mira's living presence smothered her sister Haseki Sultan Bergüzar Hatun's power, and the nearer she came the more it was muted. Osman had hidden the diviner on a farm near the old dig, erased even his own memory of the place—and now the party alone would decide whether Mira should be delivered to the Padishah, or kept far from him.
